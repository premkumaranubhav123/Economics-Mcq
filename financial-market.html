The Indian Financial System

Foundation Module — UPSC loves statement-based questions on this architecture

1 · Foundation: What & Why

A financial system is the plumbing of an economy — it channels money from those who have it (savers) to those who need it (borrowers). Without it, a doctor's savings could never reach an entrepreneur's factory.

🎯 Analogy for Clarity

Think of the Financial System as a Water System: Savings are the water source. Banks, mutual funds, stock markets are the pipes. RBI, SEBI, IRDAI are the pressure regulators. Inflation is a leak. The entire economy drinks from this system.

Money Market

Short-term liquidity market — most tested area in UPSC Economics

🎯 Core Intuition

Money Market = "Overnight ATM of the Economy." When banks run short of cash for one night, they borrow here. When government needs money for 91 days, it issues T-Bills here. It's where short-term liquidity is managed — precisely by RBI.

All Instruments — Complete Guide

① Call Money & Notice Money

Call Money: Banks lend/borrow money from each other for exactly 1 day (overnight). The rate at which this happens = Call Rate.

Notice Money: Same as Call Money but for 2–14 days. No collateral needed — purely based on trust between banks.

Term Money: Interbank transactions for 15 days to 1 year.

Mental Model

Call Money = A friend borrowing ₹500 for exactly tonight. Notice Money = Same friend, but says "sometime in the next 2 weeks." Term Money = Same friend for longer.

② Treasury Bills (T-Bills)

Short-term government borrowing instruments issued by RBI on behalf of the Government of India. They are zero-coupon instruments — issued at a discount, redeemed at face value.

✅ Key Facts for UPSC

• T-Bills are Government of India instruments, NOT State Government instruments.
• They are risk-free (gilt-edged) as the government cannot default.
• They are issued at discount (not at face value) — the interest is embedded in the discount.
• Minimum denomination: ₹25,000
• T-Bills form part of banks' SLR portfolio.
• They are traded in the secondary market.

⚠ UPSC TRAP — CP vs CD
CP = issued by Corporates. CD = issued by banks. UPSC regularly reverses this.
CP is unsecured. T-Bills are risk-free (government). Both are issued at discount.
State Governments cannot issue T-Bills. They issue State Development Loans (SDLs).
Commercial Papers require credit rating. T-Bills do NOT require credit rating (government instrument).

⑤ Repo & Reverse Repo — The Most Important Tool

🏆 Single Most Important Money Market Concept

REPO (Repurchase Agreement): A bank sells government securities to RBI and agrees to repurchase them after a specified period. The interest rate paid by banks to RBI = Repo Rate. This is how banks borrow from RBI.

REVERSE REPO: RBI borrows from banks by selling securities. RBI pays interest to banks = Reverse Repo Rate. This drains liquidity.

Golden Rule: Repo Rate > Reverse Repo Rate (always).

⑥ Liquidity Adjustment Facility (LAF)

LAF is the framework through which RBI manages day-to-day liquidity in the banking system. It has multiple windows:

FacilityNatureRatePurpose
RepoInjectionRepo Rate (Policy Rate)Banks borrow from RBI (overnight)
Reverse Repo AbsorptionRepo Rate – 25 bpsBanks park excess money with RBI
MSFEmergency InjectionRepo Rate + 25 bpsEmergency overnight borrowing
SDFAbsorptionRepo Rate – 25 bpsNEW: Banks park without collateral

⑦ Marginal Standing Facility (MSF)

Introduced in 2011. MSF is the emergency window for banks. When all interbank liquidity dries up and even repo access fails, banks can borrow from RBI's MSF window at a penal rate (Repo + 25 basis points). Banks can use even their SLR securities (up to 1% of NDTL) for this borrowing.

🚨 UPSC 2015 — Frequently Tested

SDF (Standing Deposit Facility) — introduced in April 2022 Budget. It allows banks to park excess liquidity with RBI WITHOUT any collateral. This replaced the reverse repo as the floor rate of the interest rate corridor. SDF rate = Repo Rate – 25 bps = new floor of the corridor.

Interest Rate Corridor (Critical Mental Model)

🧠 The Rate Corridor Visualization

MSF Rate (Ceiling = Repo + 0.25%)
    ↑
POLICY REPO RATE (Benchmark = e.g., 6.5%)
    ↓
SDF Rate (Floor = Repo – 0.25%)

The corridor keeps overnight rates anchored. Banks will never borrow above MSF rate (too expensive) and never park below SDF rate (suboptimal). This is RBI's liquidity management genius.

Capital Market

Long-term fund raising — Primary vs Secondary Market — Bonds, Equities, G-Secs, Derivatives

🎯 Core Intuition (Sanjiv Verma)

"Requirement of capital is an absolute necessity for any economic activity to sustain. Capital Market provides a platform whereby the people who required investment and those who are willing to invest are coming together." — It is disintermediation: companies raise money DIRECTLY from public, bypassing banks.

Primary vs Secondary Market

IPO, FPO, OFS — Explained

IPO (Initial Public Offering): First time a company sells shares to the public. Fresh capital raised goes TO the company.
FPO (Follow-on Public Offer): Company already listed; sells MORE new shares. Fresh capital again goes to company.
OFS (Offer for Sale): Existing shareholders (like promoters) sell their shares. Money goes to SELLER, NOT the company.
Rights Issue: Company offers new shares to EXISTING shareholders at discounted price.
Book Building: Price discovery mechanism where investors bid within a price band. Final price = cut-off price.

⚠ UPSC TRAP — IPO vs OFS
In OFS — money goes to SELLER (existing shareholder/promoter), NOT the company. Company gets NO money.
Government's divestment via stock exchange = OFS route (money to government, not to the PSU).
QIB (Qualified Institutional Buyer) = SEBI-defined sophisticated investors (FPIs, domestic MFs, insurance companies). NOT retail investors.

🥇 Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) — High Priority

• Issued in rupees, denominated in grams of gold by RBI on behalf of GOI.
• Interest: 2.5% per annum (paid semi-annually)
• Tenure: 8 years, exit from 5th year
• Capital gains on redemption: EXEMPT if held till maturity
• Can be used as collateral for loans
• Trading on exchanges possible (secondary market)
• Eligible for SLR of banks
• Minimum: 1 gram; Maximum: 4 kg/year (individual), 20 kg (trusts/institutions)

Corporate Bonds

Issued by companies to raise long-term debt. Unlike G-Secs, these carry credit risk. Rated by credit rating agencies (CRISIL, ICRA, CARE). India's corporate bond market is underdeveloped — a major UPSC discussion point.

✅ Green Bonds / ESG — Modern Priority

Green Bonds: Funds raised exclusively for climate/environmental projects. SEBI issued Green Bond Framework in 2023. India issued its first Sovereign Green Bond in 2023 (₹16,000 crore). RBI manages issuance.

Municipal Bonds

Issued by Urban Local Bodies (municipalities) to fund infrastructure. Pune Municipal Corporation issued India's first municipal bond in 2017 under SEBI framework. These are tax-free in the US but taxable in India.

Derivatives Market

Foundation

A derivative is a contract whose value is derived from an underlying asset (shares, commodities, currency, interest rates). You're not buying the asset — you're betting on its price movement. Used for HEDGING risk or SPECULATION.

⚠ 2008 Global Crisis Link — Derivatives
The 2008 global financial crisis was caused by complex derivatives called CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations) and Credit Default Swaps (CDS) based on subprime mortgages.
India survived 2008 better because Indian banks had limited exposure to complex derivatives and had a stronger regulatory framework by RBI.
Derivatives in India: NSE's F&O segment is one of the largest derivatives markets in the world by volume.

Circuit Breakers

Automatic halts in market trading to prevent panic-driven crashes. Triggered when Nifty 50 falls by 10%, 15%, 20% in a single day. SEBI mechanism to prevent "flash crashes."

RBI & Monetary Policy Committee

Apex monetary authority — Most tested area for UPSC PYQs

Foundation: RBI's Role

🏛 Who is RBI?

Reserve Bank of India — established April 1, 1935 under RBI Act 1934. Initially private, nationalized in 1949. Headquartered in Mumbai (initially Calcutta). Governor = chief of RBI. Currently fully owned by Government of India.

RBI plays four critical roles:
1. Monetary Authority — controls money supply & inflation
2. Banker to Government — manages government debt, treasury operations
3. Banker to Banks — lender of last resort
4. Regulator of Financial System — oversees banks, NBFCs, payment systems

Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)

June 2016
RBI Act amended to create the MPC under Section 45ZA
Target Set
Inflation target: 4% ± 2% (CPI-based), notified by Central Government every 5 years
Composition
6 members: 3 from RBI (Governor + Deputy Governor + 1 RBI official) + 3 external members appointed by GOI
Meetings
Minimum 4 meetings per year (now 6 times — bi-monthly)
Voting
Majority vote; Governor has casting vote in case of tie. Quorum = 4 members (Governor must be one)
Minutes
Published after 14 days of meeting. Each member must publish their reasoning.
Accountability
If inflation misses target for 3 consecutive quarters → RBI must submit report to Parliament with reasons + remedial action
⚠ MPC UPSC TRAPS
External members' term = 4 years, NOT 3 years or 5 years.
MPC decides only the Repo Rate (Policy Rate). Other rates (CRR, SLR) are decided by RBI independently.
MPC decision is binding on RBI. The Governor cannot override MPC.
MPC target is CPI-based, NOT WPI. This shift happened from 2016.
Government can convey its views in writing to MPC, but cannot vote or control its decisions.

Complete Monetary Policy Toolkit

① Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR)

Every bank must maintain a certain % of its Net Demand and Time Liabilities (NDTL) as cash with RBI. This money earns NO interest. Raising CRR drains liquidity; reducing CRR injects liquidity.

⚡ Impact

1% increase in CRR on ₹180 lakh crore deposit base = ₹1.8 lakh crore sucked out from banking system overnight. Extremely powerful tool — used rarely for this reason.

② Statutory Liquidity Ratio (SLR)

Every bank must invest a certain % of NDTL in government-approved securities (G-Secs, Gold, Cash). Current SLR = 18% (effective 2017). Banks get interest on SLR securities (unlike CRR). SLR ensures banks have liquid assets and also funds government borrowing.

ToolWhere KeptInterest Earned?Current Rate (approx)Use
CRRCash with RBI❌ No4.00%Liquidity control
SLRG-Secs/Gold/Cash✅ Yes (on G-Secs)18.00%Liquidity + Govt borrowing

③ Open Market Operations (OMO)

RBI buys or sells government securities in the secondary market. Voluntary (not binding on banks unlike CRR/SLR).
OMO Purchase (banks sell G-Secs to RBI) → money goes to banks → liquidity increases
OMO Sale (RBI sells G-Secs to banks) → money comes from banks → liquidity decreases

④ Bank Rate

The rate at which RBI lends to banks against eligible securities. Historically used for liquidity management. Now largely penal rate for CRR/SLR violations. Bank Rate = MSF Rate = Repo + 0.25% (linked).

⑤ Qualitative Tools (Selective Credit Control)

• Margin Requirements: On loans against commodities (prevents speculation)
• Credit Rationing: Setting credit ceilings for specific sectors
• Moral Suasion: RBI persuades banks through guidelines and advisories
• Direct Action: Penalties, license cancellation for non-compliance

Printing of Currency — Key Facts

• Notes are printed at RBI printing presses (Nashik, Dewas, Mysuru, Salboni)
• Coins are minted at Government Mints under Ministry of Finance
• Basis of printing: Minimum Reserve System — minimum gold worth ₹200 crore + government securities
• ₹1 note: Signed by Finance Secretary (not RBI Governor)
• All other notes: Signed by RBI Governor

⚠ ₹1 NOTE TRAP — Extremely Famous UPSC Question
₹1 note is issued by the Government of India (Ministry of Finance), signed by Finance Secretary. All other denominations issued by RBI, signed by Governor.
Coins are issued by Government of India, NOT RBI. RBI only issues notes.

✅ Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR / CRAR) Formula

CAR = (Tier I Capital + Tier II Capital) / Risk-Weighted Assets × 100

Tier I Capital (Core Capital): Equity capital + Retained profits = most reliable
Tier II Capital (Supplementary): Revaluation reserves + Long-term bonds (subordinated debt) + Provisions
Rule: Tier II cannot exceed Tier I. India's minimum CAR = 9% (vs BIS 8%).

Non-Performing Assets (NPAs)

🚨 NPA Definition

A loan where interest/principal is overdue for 90 days or more. Banks must classify as NPA and make provisions.

NPA CategoryDurationProvision Required
Sub-Standard AssetNPA for ≤ 12 months15% of outstanding
Doubtful AssetSub-standard for > 12 months25–100% (increases with age)
Loss AssetUncollectible, little value100% write-off

Twin Balance Sheet Problem: Overleveraged corporates + Bad-loan-laden banks = India's NPA crisis (2015–19). Economic Survey 2016–17 proposed PARA (Public Sector Asset Rehabilitation Agency) — India's version of a "Bad Bank." Later, National Asset Reconstruction Company (NARCL) was set up in 2021 — the actual "Bad Bank."

SARFAESI Act 2002

Enables banks to recover loans from defaulters WITHOUT going to court. Banks can seize and auction mortgaged property. Applies when loan > ₹1 lakh and secured. Created Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARCs) and Securitization Companies (SCs).

Key SEBI Concepts for UPSC

Insider Trading

Trading in securities based on unpublished price-sensitive information (UPSI). E.g., a company director buying shares before a positive earnings announcement. SEBI PIT (Prohibition of Insider Trading) Regulations 2015 — strict penalties including jail term.

Market Manipulation

Pump and dump: Artificially inflate a stock's price (pump), then sell at peak (dump). Circular trading: Two parties trade with each other artificially to show high volumes. Both are SEBI violations.

Mutual Fund Regulation

All mutual funds in India must be registered with SEBI. They must be set up as trusts. The Asset Management Company (AMC) manages the fund. AMFI (Association of Mutual Funds in India) is the industry body.

FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment)

Short-term, liquid investments by foreign investors in Indian equity and debt markets. Also called "hot money." Registered with SEBI. RBI sets aggregate limits. FPI > 10% ownership in a company = reclassified as FDI.

⚠ SEBI UPSC TRAPS
SEBI was non-statutory until 1992. Established 1988, statutory from 1992.
SEBI regulates mutual funds AND stock exchanges. RBI regulates banks. NOT interchangeable.
FMC merged into SEBI in 2015 — so MCX, NCDEX now under SEBI.
Collective Investment Schemes (CIS) that are NOT registered with SEBI are illegal (many chit fund scams use this route).
The SEBI SCORES platform is for investor complaint redressal — not for stock trading.

🇮🇳 India's Exchange Rate Journey (Sanjiv Verma)

1. Post-Independence: Fixed rate (₹ vs Pound Sterling)
2. Later: Basket of currencies (fixed)
3. 1991: LERMS (Liberalized Exchange Rate Management System) — dual rate (40:60)
4. FEDAI (Foreign Exchange Dealers Association of India) set up for market rate
5. 1999: RBI abandoned official rate → fully market-determined (managed float)
6. Now: Managed exchange rate — RBI intervenes to prevent excessive volatility

Impact of Currency Depreciation on India

Good for: Exports (become cheaper globally), NRI remittances (more ₹ per $)
Bad for: Imports (oil, machinery become expensive) → import inflation → CAD widens → rupee falls further (vicious cycle)
India's Specific Problem: 80%+ of oil is imported. Rupee depreciation = fuel price rise = cost-push inflation across economy.

The Impossible Trinity (Trilemma)

No country can simultaneously have all three: (1) Fixed exchange rate + (2) Free capital flows + (3) Independent monetary policy. At best, any two are possible.

🧠 The Trilemma — Examples

• China: Fixed rate + Independent monetary policy → BUT restricts capital flows
• USA: Free capital flows + Independent monetary policy → BUT free-floating exchange rate
• Eurozone: Fixed rate + Free capital flows → BUT gave up independent monetary policy (ECB runs it for all)
• India: Managed rate + Independent monetary policy → BUT limited capital account convertibility

🔴 CAD — India's Structural Problem

India runs a Current Account Deficit (CAD) — imports consistently exceed exports. In 2012–13, CAD touched 4.8% of GDP (crisis level). Sustainable level = below 2.5% of GDP. CAD is funded by capital inflows (FDI, FPI, ECBs). If capital inflows reverse → forex crisis risk.

Convertibility

Current Account Convertibility: Freedom to convert home currency for trade in goods & services. India has FULL current account convertibility since 1994 (Article VIII of IMF).
Capital Account Convertibility (CAC): Freedom to convert for capital transactions (investment flows). India does NOT have full CAC — it is partial/managed.
Tarapore Committee (1997 & 2006) recommended moving toward CAC with preconditions (fiscal discipline, low inflation, strong banking). Not implemented fully.

⚠ Convertibility UPSC TRAP
India has FULL current account convertibility. Partial capital account convertibility.
Market-determined exchange rate ≠ Convertibility. They are separate, independent concepts.
FEMA 1999 replaced FERA 1973. FERA was criminal; FEMA is civil law (less harsh).
NDF (Non-Deliverable Forwards) — offshore speculation on INR in markets like Singapore/Dubai OUTSIDE RBI control.

CBDC — Central Bank Digital Currency

🔥 Hot Topic — Digital Rupee

CBDC is a digital form of a country's fiat currency, issued and backed by the central bank. Unlike cryptocurrency, it is legal tender and centralized.

India launched Digital Rupee (e₹) in 2022:
• e₹-W (Wholesale) — for interbank settlements, launched Nov 2022
• e₹-R (Retail) — for general public use, launched Dec 2022 (pilot)
• Issued by RBI; distributed through banks
• Not the same as UPI or wallet money — it IS money (liability of RBI, not banks)

UPI Ecosystem

✅ UPI — Unified Payments Interface

• Launched by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) in 2016
• Regulated by RBI
• Works 24×7, instant bank-to-bank transfer via Virtual Payment Address (VPA)
• NPCI also runs: IMPS, NEFT, RTGS (clearing), RuPay cards, FASTag, Aadhaar Payments
• BHIM (Bharat Interface for Money) = government's UPI app
• UPI One World: For foreign visitors to India (no Indian bank account needed)
• UPI Credit Line: Link credit card to UPI — now allowed

Account Aggregator Framework

RBI-regulated entities that allow individuals to securely share their financial data (bank accounts, insurance, mutual funds) digitally with lenders, with the customer's consent. Enables data-driven lending especially for MSMEs. Key principle: "Financial data portability."

ONDC (Open Network for Digital Commerce)

Government initiative to create an open, interoperable e-commerce network — like UPI but for commerce. Aims to break the dominance of Amazon/Flipkart. Not a regulator or institution — it is a protocol/network.

Open Banking

Banks must share customer data with third-party financial service providers (with customer consent) through APIs. Enables FinTech firms to build financial products on top of bank data.

Easy
1. Which of the following is an instrument of Money Market?
  • (a) Equity shares
  • (b) Debentures
  • (c) Commercial Paper
  • (d) Government Bonds (10-year)
Answer: (c) Commercial Paper

Explanation: Money Market instruments have maturity < 1 year. CP: 7 days–1 year ✅. Equity shares = Capital market. Debentures = Capital market (long-term). 10-year G-Bonds = Capital market.

Elimination trick: Any instrument with maturity > 1 year or ownership-based = Capital Market. Duration is the key filter.
Moderate
2. Consider the following statements about Repo Rate:
1. It is the rate at which banks borrow from RBI
2. An increase in Repo Rate increases liquidity in the economy
3. It serves as the Policy Rate for monetary policy
Which of the statements given above is/are correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 1 and 3 only
  • (c) 2 and 3 only
  • (d) 1, 2 and 3
Answer: (b) 1 and 3 only

Statement 1: ✅ Correct — Repo rate = rate banks pay to borrow from RBI.
Statement 2: ❌ Wrong — Increasing Repo Rate REDUCES liquidity (makes borrowing expensive, credit contracts).
Statement 3: ✅ Correct — MPC sets the Repo Rate as the key policy rate.

UPSC Trap: Statement 2 is the classic reversal trap. Higher Repo = tighter liquidity, not more.

3. Which of the following statements about Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) is/are correct?
1. They are issued in rupees but denominated in grams of gold
2. Capital gains on redemption at maturity are exempt from tax
3. They can be used as collateral for loans
4. They are issued by SEBI on behalf of Government of India
  • (a) 1, 2 and 3 only
  • (b) 1 and 2 only
  • (c) 2, 3 and 4 only
  • (d) 1, 2, 3 and 4
Answer: (a) 1, 2 and 3 only

Statement 4 is WRONG — SGBs are issued by RBI (not SEBI) on behalf of Government of India.
SEBI ≠ Bond issuer. SEBI is capital market regulator. RBI manages government securities/bonds.

Elimination: Whenever "SEBI issues government bonds" appears — it's WRONG. RBI issues G-Secs and SGBs.

4. With reference to the Monetary Policy Committee (MPC), consider the following statements:
1. The MPC has 6 members, three each from RBI and Government of India
2. The MPC decides the Cash Reserve Ratio (CRR)
3. In case of a tie, the RBI Governor has the casting vote
4. Quorum for an MPC meeting is four members
Which of the statements given above is/are NOT correct?
  • (a) 1 and 2 only
  • (b) 2 only
  • (c) 1, 2 and 4

Answer: (b) 2 only

Statement 1: ✅ Correct — 3 from RBI (Governor, Dy Governor, Official) + 3 appointed by GOI = 6.

1. Foreign Currency Assets (FCAs)
2. Gold reserves
3. Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)

Select the correct answer:

  • (b) 1, 2 and 3 only

Moderate
8. Which statement about SDR (Special Drawing Right) is INCORRECT?
  • (a) SDR is an international reserve asset created by IMF in 1969
  • (b) SDR is a currency that can be directly used for international trade payments
  • (c) Chinese RMB (Yuan) was added to SDR basket in 2016
  • (d) SDR can be exchanged for freely usable currencies at IMF
Answer: (b)

SDR is NOT a currency. It is an international reserve asset / accounting unit. It cannot be directly used for international trade. It can only be exchanged for member countries' currencies through the IMF.

Statements (a), (c), (d) are all correct. The keyword trap: "paper gold" ≠ actual currency.
Hard
9. With reference to Sterilization in foreign exchange management, which of the following best describes it?
  • (a) RBI's purchase of foreign currency from market to prevent rupee appreciation
  • (b) RBI simultaneously absorbing excess rupee liquidity (through reverse repo/OMO) after purchasing foreign currency
  • (c) The process of converting current account surplus into capital account surplus
  • (d) Imposing restrictions on foreign currency outflows to protect reserves
Answer: (b)

When RBI buys USD → releases rupees into system → excess liquidity → inflation risk. Sterilization = simultaneously absorbing those excess rupees (via reverse repo, OMO sale) so that forex intervention doesn't cause inflation. The goal: maintain export competitiveness WITHOUT causing inflation.

Option (a) = only the forex purchase part (not sterilization itself). Sterilization = the COMPLETE operation including the liquidity absorption step.
UPSC Level
10. The "Impossible Trinity" or "Trilemma" in international economics states that a country cannot simultaneously have which three of the following?
1. Fixed exchange rate
2. Free capital mobility
3. Independent monetary policy
4. Low inflation
5. Current account surplus
  • (a) 1, 2 and 4
  • (b) 2, 3 and 5
  • (c) 1, 2 and 3
  • (d) 1, 3 and 5
Answer: (c) 1, 2 and 3

The Impossible Trinity (Mundell-Fleming Trilemma): You can have any TWO but NOT all three of:
• Fixed exchange rate
• Free capital mobility (open capital account)
• Independent monetary policy

Low inflation and current account surplus are NOT part of the trilemma definition.
