Foundation of External Sector
CORE CONCEPTS
🌍 What is the External Sector? Fundamental

The external sector refers to all economic transactions between a country and the rest of the world. It encompasses trade in goods & services, financial flows, and transfer payments crossing national borders.

💡
Intuitive Analogy: Think of a country like a household. Just as a household earns income and buys goods from outside, a country earns from exports and spends on imports. The external sector is the country's "economic relationship with the neighbourhood."
🚀 Why Countries Trade
  • No country is self-sufficient — every economy has limited resources
  • Comparative Advantage — produce what you're relatively better at
  • Economies of scale — larger production lowers costs
  • Variety gains — consumers access more products
  • Technology diffusion — trade spreads innovation
⚖️ Comparative vs Absolute Advantage
ConceptMeaningBy
Absolute AdvantageProduce more with same inputsAdam Smith
Comparative AdvantageLower opportunity costDavid Ricardo
✅
UPSC loves Comparative Advantage — it explains why even a less productive country gains from trade!
📊 Terms of Trade (ToT) UPSC Favourite

Terms of Trade = (Price Index of Exports / Price Index of Imports) × 100

ToT MovementMeaningImpact on India
ToT Improves (rises)Exports buy more importsFavorable — better purchasing power
ToT Deteriorates (falls)Must export more to buy same importsUnfavorable — oil price rise hurts India
⚠️
India-specific: India is a net oil importer — when crude prices rise, India's ToT deteriorates, worsening the Current Account Deficit (CAD).

Balance of Payments (BoP)
HIGH PRIORITY
📑 Structure of Balance of Payments Most Important

BoP is a systematic record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and the rest of the world during a given period (usually one year).

📦 Current Account Components
  • Merchandise Trade: Physical goods (exports − imports)
  • Services Trade: IT, tourism, transport, financial services
  • Primary Income: Returns on investments (dividends, interest)
  • Secondary Income: Remittances, grants, transfers
🇮🇳
India is the world's top remittance receiver (~$125 billion in 2023-24). Remittances help reduce CAD significantly.
💰 Financial Account Components
  • FDI: Foreign Direct Investment (long-term, stable)
  • FPI/FII: Portfolio investment (equity + bonds, volatile)
  • ECB: External Commercial Borrowings
  • NRI Deposits: FCNR(B), NRE, NRO accounts
  • Reserve Assets: Changes in RBI's forex reserves
🔴 Current Account Deficit (CAD) — Deep Dive Critical

CAD = Imports of Goods + Services + Primary Income outflows − Exports of Goods + Services + Primary Income inflows − Net Secondary Income

FactorEffect on CADWhy?
🛢️ Crude oil price riseWidens CADIndia imports ~85% crude needs
📉 Export slowdownWidens CADLess forex inflow
💱 Rupee depreciationMixed — widens initially, narrows long-termJ-Curve effect
💸 High remittancesNarrows CADInflows on current account
🖥️ Software exports riseNarrows CADServices credit rises
💎 Gold import surgeWidens CADIndia is world's 2nd largest gold importer
🚨 UPSC Trap — BoP vs Trade Balance
  • Trade Balance / Merchandise Trade Balance = Only physical goods (visible trade)
  • Current Account Balance = Goods + Services + Income + Transfers (includes invisible trade)
  • BoP = Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account (always = 0)
  • A country can have a trade deficit but current account surplus if services surplus is large enough (e.g., India's IT exports help)
📈 J-Curve Effect — Understanding Rupee Depreciation & CAD
▼

When rupee depreciates, one would expect CAD to narrow (exports become cheaper for foreigners, imports costlier for Indians). But in reality, CAD initially worsens before improving — this is the J-Curve effect.

📉
Why? Contracts are fixed in the short run. Import quantities don't fall immediately even though prices rise. Export quantities don't rise immediately. Over time, as volume adjusts, CAD improves — making the shape of a "J" on a graph.

🔄 Why Rupee Depreciates
  • High CAD — dollar demand exceeds supply
  • High crude oil prices — India buys more dollars
  • FPI outflows — hot money exits India
  • Strong US dollar globally
  • High inflation in India vs US
  • Geopolitical uncertainty
🏦 RBI Intervention in Forex Market

India follows a managed float — RBI intervenes to prevent excessive volatility but doesn't target a specific level.

📈
To STRENGTHEN Rupee: RBI sells dollars (draws from forex reserves) → reduces dollar supply → rupee appreciates
📉
To WEAKEN Rupee: RBI buys dollars → adds to reserves → rupee depreciates (to help exports)
⚡ Imported Inflation Current Affairs Link

When rupee depreciates, the cost of imports in rupee terms rises. Since India imports significant amounts of crude oil, edible oils, fertilizers, and electronics, rupee depreciation fuels domestic inflation — called imported inflation.

🔗
Chain: Rupee depreciates → Crude oil costlier in INR → Transport costs rise → Food inflation rises → WPI/CPI inflation rises → RBI tightens monetary policy
🚨 Key UPSC Traps — Exchange Rates
  • Trap 1: "Rupee depreciation is always bad" — FALSE. It benefits exporters and can reduce CAD over time
  • Trap 2: Confusing depreciation (market) with devaluation (policy) — always check the mechanism
  • Trap 3: "Fixed exchange rate is stable" — Stable but creates other problems (balance of payments crisis if rate is wrong)
  • Trap 4: India has a "floating" exchange rate — WRONG. It's "managed float" / "dirty float"
🧠 Mnemonic: DEPRECIATION = MARKET, DEVALUATION = DECISION When the market decides, it's depreciation. When the government decides, it's devaluation. Same direction, different driver.

🌐 What is SDR (Special Drawing Rights)? — Often Confused in UPSC
▼

SDR is NOT a currency. It is an international reserve asset created by the IMF. Its value is based on a basket of 5 currencies:

US Dollar (43.38%) Euro (29.31%) Chinese Yuan (12.28%) Japanese Yen (7.59%) British Pound (7.44%)
⭐
Key Point: Chinese Yuan was added to SDR basket in October 2016 — this is a recurring PYQ topic!
🛡️ Why India Maintains Large Forex Reserves
  • Buffer against BoP crisis — 1991 was a lesson
  • Import cover — ideally 3 months minimum
  • Currency stabilization — RBI intervention capacity
  • Confidence signal — for foreign investors
  • External debt servicing — repay dollar debts
  • National security — geopolitical leverage
  • Counters speculative attacks on rupee
  • Post-COVID — India built reserves as buffer
🆘
Lesson from Crises: Sri Lanka 2022 — Forex reserves fell to $0. Could not pay for oil/food imports → economic collapse. Pakistan 2023 — IMF bailout after reserves crashed. India 1991 — Pledged gold to Bank of England. These examples show WHY reserves matter.

Convertibility of Rupee
IMPORTANT
🔄 Current vs Capital Account Convertibility High Priority
TypeMeaningIndia's Status
Current Account Convertibility (CAC)Rupee freely convertible for trade in goods & services, remittances, travelFULL — since August 1994
Capital Account Convertibility (KAC)Rupee freely convertible for capital flows (FDI, FPI, loans)PARTIAL — not yet full
📋 Tarapore Committee — Landmark for UPSC PYQ Favourite

The Tarapore Committee on Capital Account Convertibility (1997) examined the conditions for full KAC. It set preconditions:

  • Fiscal deficit reduced to 3.5% of GDP
  • Inflation rate at 3–5%
  • Gross NPA of banking system below 5%
  • Adequate forex reserves
⚠️
Second Tarapore Committee (2006) revisited the issue but India still hasn't moved to full KAC. Asian Financial Crisis 1997 showed dangers of premature capital account openness.
🚨 UPSC Trap — Convertibility
  • India has full CURRENT account convertibility — confirmed since 1994
  • India does NOT have full Capital account convertibility
  • Full KAC means unlimited capital can flow in/out — this is risky (hot money, currency crisis)
  • Countries that rushed to KAC (Thailand, Indonesia) suffered in 1997 Asian Financial Crisis

Trade Agreements — Hierarchy & Types
ARCHITECTURE
📊 Trade Creation vs Trade Diversion PYQ Repeated
✅
Trade Creation (Good): When a country shifts from domestic high-cost production to cheaper imports from a partner country. Example: India signs FTA with country that produces steel cheaper → India imports steel → welfare improves.
⚠️
Trade Diversion (Bad): When a country switches from a low-cost non-member to a higher-cost partner (because of preferential tariffs). Example: Due to ASEAN FTA, India buys expensive ASEAN goods instead of cheaper Chinese goods → welfare loss.
🔴 Why India Walked Out of RCEP (2019)
▼

RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership) includes ASEAN + China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand. India's concerns:

  • China flood risk: Cheap Chinese goods entering India via tariff elimination
  • Trade deficit concern: India already has large trade deficits with RCEP members
  • Dairy & agriculture: New Zealand/Australia's cheap dairy would hurt India's farmers
  • Ratchet clause: India couldn't increase tariffs even if hurt
  • Rules of origin: Worry about Chinese goods re-routed through ASEAN
🇮🇳
RCEP was signed by 15 countries (without India) on November 15, 2020. It is the world's largest trade bloc by GDP and population.

WTO & Global Trade Issues
VERY HIGH PRIORITY
🌐 WTO — Structure & Key Agreements Must Know

Core Agreements

  • GATT — General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (goods)
  • GATS — General Agreement on Trade in Services
  • TRIPS — Trade-Related Intellectual Property Rights
  • TRIMS — Trade-Related Investment Measures
  • SPS Agreement — Sanitary & phytosanitary
  • TBT Agreement — Technical Barriers to Trade

Key WTO Principles

  • MFN — Most Favoured Nation (treat all equally)
  • National Treatment — Treat imports = domestic goods
  • Reciprocity — Mutual concessions
  • Transparency — Notify all trade measures
  • Dispute Settlement — Binding arbitration (DSB)
🌾 India's Agriculture Subsidy Dispute at WTO — Critical for UPSC
▼
  • 10% cap rule: WTO allows countries to provide minimum support to agriculture up to 10% of the total value of agricultural production (Aggregate Measurement of Support — AMS)
  • India's MSP issue: India's MSP for rice/wheat may breach this limit, technically violating WTO rules
  • Peace Clause: At Bali (2013), WTO agreed to a "Peace Clause" — India won't be taken to dispute settlement for food security programs while permanent solution is found
  • India's position: Food security is a sovereign right; developing countries need policy space
  • Nairobi Package 2015: Developed countries to eliminate export subsidies for agriculture
🔬 TRIPS & Doha Declaration — India vs Pharma MNCs Important

TRIPS gives pharmaceutical companies 20-year patent protection. Developing countries feared this would make life-saving drugs unaffordable.

Doha Declaration 2001 on TRIPS and Public Health: Confirmed that TRIPS allows countries to issue compulsory licenses for medicines in public health emergencies. India successfully negotiated Section 3(d) of its Patents Act to prevent ever-greening of pharma patents.

🌿 Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) — New & Critical 2024-25 CA

The EU's CBAM is a carbon tax on imports from countries with weaker climate policies. From 2026, EU importers must pay for carbon content of goods from sectors like steel, cement, fertilizers, aluminium.

🇮🇳
India's concern: CBAM is seen as a trade barrier. India exports significant amounts of steel and other CBAM-covered goods to EU. Could cost Indian exporters billions. India argues it's discriminatory and violates WTO's non-discrimination principles.
🔄 Supply Chain Shifts — China+1, Friendshoring High Probability
🇨🇳+1
China+1 Strategy: MNCs diversifying manufacturing away from China to reduce dependence. India, Vietnam, Bangladesh, Mexico are beneficiaries. India's PLI scheme targets this opportunity.
🤝
Friendshoring: Routing supply chains through geopolitically aligned countries. US-led trend post-COVID and Ukraine war. India benefits from being a US strategic partner.

🔄 Sterilization — A Critical Concept Often Confused
▼

When RBI buys dollars (to prevent rupee from appreciating), it releases rupees into the system → increases money supply → risks inflation.

To prevent this, RBI sterilizes the intervention by selling government securities (G-Secs) in open market, absorbing the excess rupee liquidity.

⚡
Sterilized vs Unsterilized Intervention:
Sterilized = Neutral on money supply | Unsterilized = Expands money supply
🚨 Important: 2013 Rupee Crisis
  • May 2013: Fed "Taper Tantrum" — Bernanke signaled US tapering QE
  • FPIs pulled money from emerging markets → Rupee crashed to ₹68/$
  • RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan launched FCNR(B) swap scheme → attracted $26 billion from NRIs → stabilized rupee
  • This episode shows: FPI outflows are India's biggest external vulnerability

Q.01 Easy Foundation
Which of the following is the CORRECT formula for Terms of Trade?
A (Import Price Index / Export Price Index) × 100
B (Export Price Index / Import Price Index) × 100
C (Total Exports / Total Imports) × 100
D (Current Account / Capital Account) × 100
✅ Answer: B

Terms of Trade = (Export Price Index / Import Price Index) × 100. If ToT > 100, it's favorable (exports can buy more imports). India's ToT is sensitive to crude oil prices since India is a major oil importer.

❌ Option A is the inverse — would give the "Import Terms of Trade", not the standard ToT.
❌ Option C mixes up values with price indices.
Q.02 Medium BoP
Consider the following statements regarding India's Current Account Deficit (CAD):

1. Remittances from Indians abroad are recorded on the current account and help reduce CAD.
2. Software export earnings are recorded under the capital account.
3. Crude oil imports are the largest contributor to India's merchandise trade deficit.

Which of the above statements is/are correct?
A 1 only
B 1 and 2 only
C 1 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
✅ Answer: C (1 and 3 only)

Statement 1 — CORRECT: Remittances are recorded under "Secondary Income" in the current account. They are inflows and reduce CAD. India receives ~$125 billion annually.

Statement 2 — WRONG: Software/IT exports are services exports. Services are part of the CURRENT ACCOUNT (invisible trade), NOT capital account. This is a classic UPSC trap!

Statement 3 — CORRECT: Crude oil imports are India's single largest import, consistently the biggest contributor to merchandise trade deficit.
Q.03 Hard PYQ Style
With reference to India's exchange rate regime, which of the following statements is correct?
A India follows a fixed exchange rate system where the RBI pegs the rupee to the US dollar
B India follows a freely floating exchange rate with no RBI intervention whatsoever
C India follows a managed floating exchange rate where RBI intervenes to prevent excessive volatility
D The rupee is pegged to a basket of currencies including USD, EUR, and GBP
✅ Answer: C

India's exchange rate system is called "Managed Float" or "Dirty Float." The rupee is determined by market forces (demand & supply) but the RBI reserves the right to intervene to prevent excessive or disorderly movements. RBI does NOT target a specific rupee level.

❌ A — Fixed system was pre-1991. India moved away from fixed rates.
❌ B — Completely false. RBI regularly buys/sells dollars.
❌ D — Rupee is NOT pegged to any basket. That's different from currency unions.
Q.04 Hard PYQ Style
Which among the following are components of India's Forex Reserves?

1. Foreign Currency Assets
2. Gold
3. Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)
4. Reserve Tranche Position with IMF

Select the correct answer using the codes below:
A 1 and 2 only
B 1, 2 and 3 only
C 1, 2, 3 and 4
D 2, 3 and 4 only
✅ Answer: C — All four are components

India's forex reserves have FOUR components:
1. Foreign Currency Assets (FCA) — ~94% of reserves
2. Gold — physical gold at RBI
3. SDRs — IMF's Special Drawing Rights allocation
4. Reserve Tranche Position (RTP) — India's unconditional drawing rights at IMF

All four are reported weekly by RBI. This is a direct recall question — all four components must be memorized.
Q.05 Medium SDR
Special Drawing Rights (SDR) of the IMF is valued based on a basket of currencies. Which of the following is CORRECT about SDR?

1. Chinese Yuan was included in the SDR basket in 2016.
2. SDR is a currency that can be used directly for international transactions.
3. SDR allocation is proportional to a country's quota at IMF.

Select the correct answer:
A 1 only
B 1 and 3 only
C 2 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
✅ Answer: B (1 and 3 only)

Statement 1 — CORRECT: Chinese Yuan (Renminbi) was added to the SDR basket in October 2016, making it a 5-currency basket (USD, EUR, CNY, JPY, GBP).

Statement 2 — WRONG: SDR is NOT a currency. It cannot be used directly in transactions. It is a reserve asset and unit of account. SDR must be exchanged for actual currencies through voluntary trades or IMF arrangements.

Statement 3 — CORRECT: SDR allocation/quota is based on a member country's quota in IMF, which reflects its economic size and openness.
Q.06 Hard WTO
Consider the following statements about the WTO Peace Clause for agriculture:

1. It provides permanent protection to developing countries from WTO disputes over food security programs.
2. It was agreed upon at the Bali Ministerial Conference in 2013.
3. India's procurement of food at Minimum Support Price (MSP) triggered its invocation.

Which of the above statements is/are correct?
A 1 and 2 only
B 2 and 3 only
C 1 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
✅ Answer: B (2 and 3 only)

Statement 1 — WRONG: The Peace Clause is a TEMPORARY protection, NOT permanent. It's in place until a permanent solution is found. India wants the temporary clause converted to a permanent provision.

Statement 2 — CORRECT: Agreed at Bali (December 2013) at the WTO Ministerial Conference — India's major diplomatic win.

Statement 3 — CORRECT: India's rice and wheat procurement at MSP (above market price) can cross WTO's 10% AMS cap. Peace Clause prevents India from being challenged at WTO DSB for this.
Q.07 Medium Capital Flows
Which of the following best describes the difference between Currency Depreciation and Currency Devaluation?
A Depreciation occurs in a fixed exchange rate system; Devaluation occurs in a floating system
B Both terms mean exactly the same thing — fall in the value of a currency
C Depreciation is market-driven fall in currency value; Devaluation is a deliberate government/central bank action
D Devaluation means the domestic currency becomes stronger; Depreciation makes it weaker
✅ Answer: C

This is one of the most tested distinctions in UPSC economy:
Depreciation = Market forces (demand-supply of forex) determine currency falls. No government action.
Devaluation = Government/central bank DELIBERATELY lowers the currency's exchange rate. Used under fixed/pegged systems.

India's current system (managed float) sees depreciation if rupee falls due to market forces. But in 1966 and 1991, India had devaluation — official policy decision.
Q.08 Hard PYQ Style
With reference to Capital Account Convertibility (CAC) of the Indian Rupee, which of the following is correct?
A India has had full CAC since 1994 following IMF recommendation
B India has had full Current Account Convertibility but only partial Capital Account Convertibility
C The Tarapore Committee recommended full CAC without any preconditions
D The Tarapore Committee set preconditions like fiscal consolidation and NPA reduction before full CAC
✅ Answer: D

❌ Option A — India achieved full CURRENT Account Convertibility in 1994, NOT Capital Account.
❌ Option B — Only the first part is correct (full CAC), but India has PARTIAL KAC, making the combined statement partially wrong.
❌ Option C — Completely wrong. Tarapore explicitly set preconditions.
✅ Option D — CORRECT: The 1997 Tarapore Committee set clear preconditions: (1) fiscal deficit ≤ 3.5% GDP, (2) inflation 3-5%, (3) bank NPA < 5%, (4) adequate forex reserves. These were to ensure macro stability before opening capital flows.
Q.09 Medium Trade Agreements
Trade creation and trade diversion are outcomes associated with regional trade agreements. Which of the following is CORRECT?
A Both trade creation and trade diversion increase economic welfare
B Trade diversion occurs when a country shifts from an expensive domestic producer to a cheaper partner country
C Trade creation increases welfare; trade diversion reduces welfare by replacing cheaper non-member imports with costlier partner imports
D India's ASEAN FTA led only to trade creation with no trade diversion
✅ Answer: C

Trade Creation: Country shifts from high-cost domestic production to low-cost partner imports → efficiency gain → welfare ↑

Trade Diversion: Country switches from low-cost non-member to high-cost partner (because of preferential tariffs on partner) → efficiency loss → welfare ↓

India's experience with ASEAN FTA involved some trade diversion — cheap ASEAN goods displaced non-ASEAN goods that were actually cheaper without tariff preferences. India's trade deficit with ASEAN widened after the FTA.
Q.10 Hard Current Affairs
With reference to the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) of the European Union, consider the following statements:

1. CBAM imposes a carbon price on certain imports to prevent 'carbon leakage'.
2. India's steel and aluminium exports to EU are among sectors covered by CBAM.
3. India has supported CBAM as it aligns with India's climate commitments.

Which of the above is/are correct?
A 1 only
B 1 and 2 only
C 2 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
✅ Answer: B (1 and 2 only)

Statement 1 — CORRECT: CBAM is designed to prevent "carbon leakage" — where EU industries move to countries with weaker climate rules, or cheap carbon-intensive imports undercut EU producers.

Statement 2 — CORRECT: CBAM covers steel, aluminium, cement, fertilizers, electricity, hydrogen. India exports significant quantities of steel and aluminium to EU.

Statement 3 — WRONG: India has OPPOSED CBAM, calling it a discriminatory trade barrier and violation of WTO's non-discrimination and "common but differentiated responsibilities" principle. India argues it punishes developing countries unfairly.

Q.12 Hard UPSC Trap
Consider the following about FDI and FPI:

1. FDI involves ownership of 10% or more of voting stock in a foreign company.
2. FPI is more stable than FDI because portfolio investors have longer time horizons.
3. FPI flows are regulated by SEBI in India, while FDI is regulated by DPIIT.

Which of the above is/are correct?
A 2 and 3 only
B 1 only
C 1 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
✅ Answer: C (1 and 3 only)

Statement 1 — CORRECT: IMF/OECD definition: FDI = acquiring 10% or more ownership (voting stock) in a foreign enterprise, implying lasting interest and control.

Statement 2 — WRONG: This is the OPPOSITE. FPI is LESS stable — it is "hot money" that can exit quickly. FDI is more stable. This is a classic reversal trap UPSC uses.

Statement 3 — CORRECT: FPI (including FIIs and QFIs) is regulated by SEBI. FDI policy is under DPIIT (Department for Promotion of Industry and Internal Trade).
